Mechanism

Figure 1:DNAwalker

We designed a system to move the DNA origami structure via a DNAWalker. First, We will summarize the reference article [1]. The walker is a structure in which two DNA strands are partially hybridized, and each single strand represents a leg. In addition, the scaffolds are designed to form partial complementary sequences to form a hairpin structure so that they do not bind, even if fuel DNA is added. However, S1 and S2 had exactly the same base sequence, and it is not known whether the Walker's reaction proceeds from the left foot or the right foot. The Walker will be dangling when it moves forward from the right foot (w2), resulting in an undesired reaction system. As a solution to this problem, we utilized mismatched base pairs. Specifically, we replaced a sequence with one that is not fully complementary to ensure that unintended chains do not bind to each other. When chains join to form a double strand, since there are many complimentary combinations in each strand, they are combined, and the combination is intentionally reduced to make it uncoupled.
The principle of the Walker with a mismatched base pair is explained below. mismatched base pairs were incorporated into the base sequence to satisfy the specifications indicated in Table 1. The symbol ◯ indicates a successful combination, and the X symbol indicates that the components are not successfully combined. For example, S1 and F1 are coupled, whereas S1 and F2 are not.

First, the left leg (W1) and the right foot (W2) are arranged in different arrangements; the Walker scaffolds are arranged in different steps, as described below, and the left leg (W1) and S1, followed by the right leg (W2) are designed. In addition, Fuel is a duplex composed of F1 and F2 designed with mismatched base pairs such that the toehold of F2 is hidden and only the toehold of F1 is exposed. F1 and S1 combine to dissociate F2 and W1, and then dissociated W1 and S2 combine to move the Walker. The detailed reaction procedure is described in 8 steps below.

Step 1

In this process, we aim to peel S1 and W1 and float the left foot (W1).

  1. initial state
  2. S1 and F1 join in the toehold region
  3. In the chain substitution reaction between S1 and F1, F2 starts to dissociate
  4. In the chain substitution reaction of W1 and F1, W1 and F2 dissociate
  5. F1 and S1 are combined, and W1 is dangling

    4. The binding of W2 and Spot 2 collapses the hairpin structure. As a result, the Walker moves one step.

    In this process, the right foot (W2) floated in Step 7 is combined with Spot 4 and the goal is to move one step further.

    1. Initial state
    2. Dangling W2 searches for complementary sequences
    3. W2 and Spot 4 join in the toehold region
    4. By combining W2 and Spot 4, Output is released. As a result, the Walker moves one step.

    In this way, by incorporating mismatched base pairs, the Walker is designed to proceed in a single direction.
